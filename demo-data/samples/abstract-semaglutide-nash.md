**Title**: Efficacy and Safety of Once-Weekly Semaglutide 2.4 mg in Adults with Biopsy-Confirmed Nonalcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH): The ESSENCE-DEMO Randomized Phase 3 Trial

**Authors**: Smith J, Wang L, Müller K, et al.

**Affiliation**: International NASH Research Consortium

---

## Background

Nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), now recognized as the progressive form of metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD), represents a significant unmet medical need with no approved pharmacotherapy in most jurisdictions as of early 2026. Semaglutide, a glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonist, has demonstrated metabolic and hepatic benefits in earlier-phase trials. This phase 3 study evaluated its efficacy and safety in patients with biopsy-confirmed NASH and stage 2 or 3 fibrosis.

## Methods

In this 72-week, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, 1,200 adults aged 18 to 75 years with histologically confirmed NASH were randomly assigned in a 2:1 ratio to receive subcutaneous semaglutide 2.4 mg once weekly or matching placebo, in addition to lifestyle counseling. The primary endpoint was resolution of NASH without worsening of fibrosis at week 72, assessed by central blinded biopsy review. Key secondary endpoints included improvement in fibrosis by at least one stage without worsening of NASH, change in body weight, and change in HbA1c. Safety was monitored through treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) and serious adverse events (SAEs).

## Results

A total of 800 patients were randomized to semaglutide and 400 to placebo; 92.4% completed week 72 assessment. NASH resolution without worsening of fibrosis was achieved in 62.9% of patients receiving semaglutide compared with 34.3% in the placebo group (difference 28.7 percentage points; 95% confidence interval [CI], 21.1 to 36.2; P<0.001). Improvement in fibrosis by at least one stage was observed in 36.8% versus 22.4% (P<0.001). Mean body weight decreased by 10.5% in the semaglutide group versus 0.8% in the placebo group (P<0.001). Mean HbA1c reduction was 1.6% versus 0.2% (P<0.001) in the subgroup with type 2 diabetes mellitus.

TEAEs were reported in 78.3% of the semaglutide group and 76.1% of the placebo group. The most common TEAEs were gastrointestinal in nature, including nausea (36.9% vs 12.1%), diarrhea (24.6% vs 11.8%), and vomiting (18.4% vs 4.2%). SAEs occurred in 8.7% versus 7.9% of patients; no treatment-related deaths were reported. Discontinuation due to adverse events occurred in 6.2% of the semaglutide group.

## Conclusions

Once-weekly semaglutide 2.4 mg led to a significantly higher rate of NASH resolution and fibrosis improvement compared with placebo at 72 weeks, with a safety profile consistent with the established GLP-1 receptor agonist class. These findings support semaglutide as a potential pharmacotherapy for NASH with fibrosis. Long-term outcomes on hepatic decompensation and major adverse cardiovascular events remain under investigation.
